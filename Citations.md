# Academic Citations — Feedback Chaos Resonator

> Bibliography of sources for tuning systems, rhythm patterns, chaos mathematics, and waveguide synthesis.
> This document accompanies the instrument and is intended for academic review, cultural accountability, and IP provenance.

## Tuning Systems

### West African Tuning & Intonation

- **Akinbo, Samuel O.** (2019). "Representation of Yoruba Tones by a Talking Drum." *Proceedings of the Annual Meetings on Phonology*. DOI: 10.3765/amp. — Frequency measurements of dundun speech surrogacy; source for pitch intervals in Yoruba tonal systems.

- **Agawu, Kofi.** (2003). *Representing African Music: Postcolonial Notes, Queries, Positions.* New York: Routledge. — Framework for discussing African musical systems without reductive "world music" framing.

- **Agawu, Kofi.** (1995). *African Rhythm: A Northern Ewe Perspective.* Cambridge: Cambridge University Press. — Timeline theory, polyrhythmic organization, bell patterns (adapted for Bembe, Shiko, Gahu patterns).

- **Nzewi, Meki.** (2007). *A Contemporary Study of Musical Arts.* Pretoria: Centre for Indigenous Instrumental African Music and Dance (CIIMDA). — Nigerian/Igbo organology, drum ensemble hierarchy (iya ilu, omele, gudugudu — source for voice naming).

- **Kubik, Gerhard.** (1999). *Africa and the Blues.* Jackson: University Press of Mississippi. — Cross-continental tuning documentation: equiheptatonic (Central/Southern Africa), equipentatonic (Uganda), Blue Note origins.

- **Kubik, Gerhard.** (2010). *Theory of African Music, Vol. 1 & 2.* Chicago: University of Chicago Press. — Comprehensive tuning system documentation including BaAka/Pygmy 5-TET, Chopi timbila tunings, inherent patterns.

### Southern & Eastern African Tuning

- **Tracey, Andrew.** (1970). "The Matepe Mbira Music of Rhodesia." *African Music* 4(4): 37-61. — Shona mbira tuning: Nyamaropa and Gandanga modes. Source for interval measurements (180, 380, 500, 700, 880, 1080 cents and variants).

- **Tracey, Hugh.** (1969). "The Mbira Class of African Instruments in Rhodesia." *African Music* 4(3). — Foundational mbira documentation; Tracey catalogue numbering system.

- **Berliner, Paul.** (1978). *The Soul of Mbira: Music and Traditions of the Shona People of Zimbabwe.* Berkeley: University of California Press. — Nyamaropa/Gandanga modes in performance context; tuning variation across regions.

- **Jones, A.M.** (1959). *Studies in African Music.* London: Oxford University Press. — Kora tuning (Silaba, Tomora modes), xylophone measurements, polyrhythmic transcription methodology.

### Kora & Mande Tuning

- **Charry, Eric.** (2000). *Mande Music: Traditional and Modern Music of the Maninka and Mandinka of Western Africa.* Chicago: University of Chicago Press. — Kora tuning systems (Silaba, Tomora, Sauta, Hardino), interval measurements, regional variation.

- **Knight, Roderic.** (1973). "Mandinka Jaliya." PhD dissertation, UCLA. — Kora modal system documentation.

### Equi-Temperament Documentation

- **Arom, Simha.** (1991). *African Polyphony and Polyrhythm.* Cambridge: Cambridge University Press. — BaAka Pygmy polyphony, Central African xylophone tuning measurements (equiheptatonic and equipentatonic), melodic construction.

- **Dehoux, Vincent & Voisin, Frédéric.** (1993). "An Interactive Experimental Method for the Determination of Musical Scales in Oral Cultures." *Contemporary Music Review* 9(1-2): 13-23. — Methodology for empirical tuning measurement; equipentatonic verification.

## Rhythm Patterns

### West African Timeline Theory

- **Toussaint, Godfried T.** (2013). *The Geometry of Musical Rhythm.* Boca Raton: CRC Press. — Euclidean rhythms, necklace theory, mathematical analysis of African bell patterns. Source for Bjorklund/Euclidean algorithm implementation.

- **Toussaint, Godfried T.** (2005). "The Euclidean Algorithm Generates Traditional Musical Rhythms." *Proceedings of BRIDGES: Mathematical Connections in Art, Music, and Science*. — Proof that Bjorklund spacing algorithm generates Sub-Saharan African rhythms.

- **Locke, David.** (1982). "Principles of Offbeat Timing and Cross-Rhythm in Southern Eve Drumming." *Ethnomusicology* 26(2): 217-246. — Cross-rhythmic structure source for polyrhythmic arp modes.

- **Pressing, Jeff.** (1983). "Cognitive Isomorphisms Between Pitch and Rhythm in World Musics." *Studies in Music* 17: 38-61. — Theoretical framework for tuning-rhythm isomorphism (relevant to the instrument's coupling of tuning and pattern systems).

### Lusophone African Rhythms

- **Moorman, Marissa J.** (2008). *Intonations: A Social History of Music and Nation in Luanda, Angola, from 1945 to Recent Times.* Athens: Ohio University Press. — Semba/Kuduro historical context.

- **Alisch, Stefanie.** (2017). *Kuduro: Accelerated Culture in Luanda, Angola.* PhD dissertation, Bayreuth University. — Kuduro rhythmic patterns, 130-140 BPM template, syncopation structure.

- **Citizen DJ / Library of Congress.** Various holdings. — Marrabenta (Mozambican) and Kizomba rhythm transcriptions used for pattern verification.

## Chaos Mathematics & Nonlinear Dynamics

- **Lorenz, Edward N.** (1963). "Deterministic Nonperiodic Flow." *Journal of the Atmospheric Sciences* 20(2): 130-141. — Original Lorenz attractor paper. Source for sigma/rho/beta parameters and ODE system.

- **Rossler, Otto E.** (1976). "An Equation for Continuous Chaos." *Physics Letters A* 57(5): 397-398. — Rossler attractor source.

- **Chua, Leon O.** (1992). "The Genesis of Chua's Circuit." *Archiv für Elektronik und Übertragungstechnik* 46(4): 250-257. — Chua circuit/attractor source; alpha/beta parameters.

- **Sprott, Julien C.** (2003). *Chaos and Time-Series Analysis.* Oxford: Oxford University Press. — Halvorsen and Aizawa attractor documentation; practical integration methods.

- **Strogatz, Steven H.** (1994). *Nonlinear Dynamics and Chaos.* Cambridge, MA: Westview Press. — Pedagogical reference for attractor behavior (convergent, onset of chaos, periodic windows at high rho).

- **Smith, Julius O.** (2010). *Physical Audio Signal Processing.* W3K Publishing. (https://ccrma.stanford.edu/~jos/pasp/) — Digital waveguide theory; feedback loop design, delay-line tuning, loss filter implementation.

- **Jaffe, David A. & Smith, Julius O.** (1983). "Extensions of the Karplus-Strong Plucked-String Algorithm." *Computer Music Journal* 7(2): 56-69. — Extended KS with brightness control (relevant to brightness parameter).

## Diaspora Visual Art Precedent

- **Lam, Wilfredo.** (1943). *The Jungle* [painting]. Museum of Modern Art, New York. — Afro-Cuban painter who formalized Vodou/Santería cosmological forms into geometric visual composition. The most direct visual art precedent for the Feedback instrument's approach: diaspora artist using African-Caribbean spiritual geometry as structural foundation for new work, not as decoration. Lam studied with Breton and Picasso but rejected their primitivist framing — he returned to Cuba and worked FROM the tradition, not about it.

- **Lam, Wilfredo.** (1944). *The Eternal Present* [painting]. Rhode Island School of Design Museum. — Lwa/orisha hybrid figures as architectural composition. Veve-adjacent geometry rendered as painting.

- **Lowery Stokes Sims.** (2002). *Wifredo Lam and the International Avant-Garde, 1923-1982.* Austin: University of Texas Press. — Definitive monograph situating Lam's Afro-Cuban cosmological formalism within (and against) European modernism.

## Ethnomusicology Methodology Notes

**On interval measurements**: All cent values in the tuning tables are approximations derived from published spectral analyses and instrument measurements. Real performance practice involves significant pitch flexibility (especially in Yoruba vocal traditions). The `flex` parameter is designed to reintroduce this flexibility computationally.

**On pattern transcriptions**: Rhythm patterns are derived from published transcriptions, not from direct recording analysis. They represent idealized forms; real performance involves micro-timing deviations. The arpeggiator's phase-based approach does not capture these deviations.

**On naming**: Voice names (iya ilu, omele, ogene, gudugudu, shekere) reference West African drum ensemble roles as a mnemonic framework. The instrument does NOT model these specific instruments. See Cultural-Context.md.
