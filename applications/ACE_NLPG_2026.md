## Project Title

Feedback: New Electronic Instruments Exploring Chaos Mathematics and African Tuning Systems

## What Is the Project?

Feedback is a suite of four linked electronic instruments built in Max/MSP that combine chaos mathematics (strange attractors) with physically-modeled resonators tuned to 19 African tonal systems. The project produces sounds that exist in no acoustic tradition — the harmonic framework comes from Yoruba, Shona, Mandinka, Chopi, and Buganda musical traditions rather than Western 12-tone equal temperament.

The primary instrument is 95% complete. This grant funds: completion of the remaining three instruments, engagement of an ethnomusicology advisor to validate the tuning data, a cultural context document ensuring responsible representation, a demo EP recording, an Ableton Live wrapper to reach the broader electronic music community, and a live performance premiere in London.

## Why Does This Matter?

Every commercial synthesizer encodes Western music theory as default. No electronic instrument offers African tuning systems as a playable option. This is not a technical oversight — it reflects whose musical knowledge is valued in instrument design.

Feedback addresses this by implementing 19 tuning systems derived from published ethnomusicological research. Each tuning is mathematically modelled from frequency measurements in peer-reviewed literature (Akinbo 2019, Kubik 2010, Tracey 1970, Charry 2000, Arom 1991, Agawu 2003). A flexible intonation parameter models the pitch variability that characterises real African musical practice.

The chaos mathematics dimension (Lorenz, Rossler, Chua, Halvorsen, Aizawa attractors) provides a genuinely novel excitation source for waveguide physical models, producing timbres impossible on conventional synthesisers. The artistic lineage runs through David Tudor, Alvin Lucier, and Eliane Radigue — canonical experimental music — but tuned to scales no electronic instrument has offered.

## How Does This Fit ACE's Investment Principles?

### Ambition and Quality
The project combines three domains that have never been connected in a single instrument: chaos mathematics, waveguide physical modelling, and ethnomusicologically-sourced tuning systems. The primary instrument (95% complete) demonstrates the concept works. The grant enables the full four-instrument suite.

### Dynamism
The project uses emerging computational techniques (real-time spectral analysis via FluCoMa, chaos oscillators at audio rate, swarm intelligence for melodic generation) to create an instrument practice that will persist beyond any single technology cycle.

### Inclusivity and Relevance
By centring African tuning systems — specifically Yoruba, Igbo, Shona, Mandinka, Chopi, and Buganda musical frameworks — the project challenges the Western-default assumptions in electronic music. A cultural context document (shipped with the instrument) ensures responsible framing, full citation, and invitation for correction from practitioners.

### Environmental Responsibility
Software instrument — no physical manufacturing, no shipping, no waste. The gen~ DSP engine runs on standard computing hardware.

## Who Will Benefit?

**Primary**: Electronic musicians, sound artists, and composers seeking alternatives to Western-default synthesis tools. Estimated addressable community: Afrofuturist/experimental electronic producers, physical modelling enthusiasts, computational ethnomusicology researchers.

**Secondary**: Ethnomusicology researchers gaining a computational platform for studying African tuning systems. Music technology educators gaining teaching material on non-Western tuning.

**Broader**: Audiences at experimental music festivals and galleries experiencing genuinely novel timbres and culturally-grounded electronic performance.
