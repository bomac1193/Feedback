## 4. Full Project Description (500 words max)

Every synthesizer ever built encodes Western music theory as its default. 12-tone equal temperament, 4/4 time signatures, chord-based harmony — these are not universal principles but one culture's system presented as the standard. Feedback challenges this by building electronic instruments whose harmonic logic comes from African musical traditions.

The project is a suite of four linked instruments built in Max/MSP:

**Instrument 1 — Chaos Resonator** uses five strange attractors (Lorenz, Rossler, Chua, Halvorsen, Aizawa) as excitation sources for six waveguide resonators, each tuned to one of 19 tuning systems derived from published ethnomusicological research. The chaos provides deterministic but unpredictable energy; the waveguides provide physically-modeled resonant bodies; the tuning systems provide harmonic frameworks from Yoruba, Shona, Mandinka, Chopi, and Buganda musical traditions. A real-time spectral analysis layer (FluCoMa) feeds back into the chaos parameters, creating a cybernetic system that listens to itself and steers its own evolution. This instrument is 95% complete and playable now.

**Instrument 2 — Corpus Resonance** takes any audio archive — field recordings, historical recordings, a festival's own archive — and uses it as excitation for the same physical models. The archive becomes the instrument. This makes site-specific and culturally-specific commissioned works possible from a single practice.

**Instrument 3 — Kuramoto Rhythm Engine** uses six coupled oscillators to produce polyrhythm from physics rather than programming. As coupling strength increases, the oscillators synchronize, sweeping from scattered polyrhythmic texture to locked groove — modelling the self-organizing principles documented in West African ensemble music.

**Instrument 4 — Swarm Polyphony** places 10-200 agents in pitch space with tuning-system gridlines as attractors. Separation, alignment, and cohesion forces produce emergent call-and-response melody — the quintessential African musical structure arising from simple rules rather than composition.

All four instruments share the same waveguide resonators and tuning systems. Each explores a different dimension of sonic possibility: the self (cybernetic feedback), the world (cultural archives), the collective (synchronization), and the ensemble (emergent melody).

The tuning systems are the project's core intellectual contribution. Each is mathematically implemented from published frequency measurements by ethnomusicologists Gerhard Kubik, Andrew Tracey, Simha Arom, Eric Charry, Samuel Akinbo, and Kofi Agawu. A flexible intonation parameter models the pitch variability that characterizes real African musical practice — this is not imprecision but a feature of these tuning traditions.

The grant would fund: an ethnomusicology advisor to validate all 19 tuning systems against measured data; completion of Instruments 3 and 4; a TouchDesigner visualization layer rendering chaos attractor trajectories as 3D visual art; a demo recording for label and festival pitches; and a cultural context document ensuring responsible framing of the source traditions.

The artistic lineage runs through David Tudor's Rainforest, Alvin Lucier's I Am Sitting in a Room, and Eliane Radigue's feedback-based practice — but tuned to scales that no electronic instrument has ever offered.

## 6. Artistic Merit (100 words max)

Feedback produces sounds impossible on any existing instrument — sub-bass waveguides at 30Hz in Yoruba Pentatonic tuning, metallic crystalline resonances in Shona Nyamaropa mode, polyrhythmic arpeggios through six heterogeneous timbres modelled on the West African drum ensemble's spectral division. The chaos-to-waveguide topology is genuinely novel: no prior instrument combines strange attractors with physical modeling resonators. The result is not a simulation of African instruments but an entirely new sonic territory, where the harmonic framework itself carries cultural meaning. The instrument's cybernetic feedback loop means each performance is genuinely emergent — the system discovers its own behaviour in real time.

## 7. Technology-Culture Dialogue (100 words max)

The project directly addresses a structural absence in music technology: the near-total lack of non-Western tuning systems in electronic instruments. By implementing 19 tuning systems from ethnomusicological research — each cited to published frequency measurements — Feedback makes African musical logic computationally playable for the first time. The technology (chaos mathematics, waveguide synthesis, real-time spectral analysis) serves as a bridge between computational musicology and living musical traditions. A cultural context document ships with the instrument, ensuring transparency about sources, limitations, and responsible use. The flexible intonation parameter encodes a fundamental insight: that African tuning is contextual and alive, not fixed.
