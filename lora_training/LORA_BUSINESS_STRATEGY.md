# LoRA business strategy: where the money is

A strategic read on the LoRA / fine-tuned weights market and what's actually defensible. Written for a creator already building a post-vanity-metric ecosystem (Tizita / Crucibla / Imperium / StanVault / Boveda).

---

## 1. Are LoRAs a profitable business in the future?

**Yes, but not the obvious way.** Selling individual LoRAs on a marketplace is a race to the bottom: foundation models keep absorbing capabilities, generic LoRAs get commoditized, hobbyists give them away free on Civitai. That market shrinks every quarter.

**The profitable business is the infrastructure around LoRAs:** licensing, provenance, royalty rails, identity protection, and access-controlled inference. Same lesson as music: selling MP3s is hard, owning the rails (Spotify, royalty admin, distribution) is profitable.

The artist who **owns the rails** for their own LoRAs and licenses them under controlled terms makes more than the artist who tries to sell .safetensors files.

## 2. The pain points worth solving

Ranked by how acute the pain is in 2026 and how willing customers are to pay for the fix.

| # | Pain point | Who has it | Pays? |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **No provenance for AI weights.** No way to prove a LoRA was trained on consented data. | Enterprises, brands, advertisers | Yes, hard |
| 2 | **No licensing infrastructure.** "How do I sell/lease a LoRA with terms enforced?" | Artists, agencies, labels | Yes, medium |
| 3 | **Identity theft via deepfake LoRAs.** Anyone can train your face from public photos. | Public figures, models, celebrities | Yes, hard |
| 4 | **No royalty rails.** When my style/face is used, I don't get paid. | Artists, IP rights holders | Yes, medium |
| 5 | **Brand-safe AI is unsolved.** Enterprises need confidence that the LoRAs they use weren't trained on stolen IP. | Fortune 500 marketing | Yes, hardest |
| 6 | **Misuse detection is manual.** When a LoRA leaks, you can't find derived content automatically. | Anyone whose face is in a LoRA | Yes, soft |
| 7 | **Discovery is broken.** Buyers can't find quality LoRAs without sifting through trash. | Production studios, agencies | Yes, soft |
| 8 | **Style consistency at scale.** Brands want every campaign asset to feel coherent. | Ad agencies, DTC brands | Yes, medium |

Pain 1 + 3 + 5 are the **enterprise tier**. They will pay 1000x what hobbyists will. Build for those, not for the Civitai crowd.

## 3. Emerging adjacent industries

These industries don't yet have a "LoRA layer" but absolutely need one.

### AI licensing (Getty Images for weights)
- Today: stock photo / footage marketplaces.
- Tomorrow: licensable LoRAs with rights-cleared training data, geographic restrictions, time-bounded contracts.
- Adobe Firefly tried this with their training-data-cleared promise. Big enterprises bought it.
- Open question: who owns the marketplace layer? Currently nobody.

### Provenance (C2PA for models)
- C2PA exists for content (images, video). Not yet for model weights.
- Imagine: every LoRA carries a cryptographic manifest of: training dataset hash, source consent attestations, fine-tune lineage from the foundation model.
- Buyers verify the manifest before licensing. Auditors verify in compliance reviews.
- Adobe + Microsoft + a few others are pushing C2PA. Weights-equivalent is open territory.

### Watermarked output / forensic provenance
- Every image generated by a registered LoRA carries a steganographic watermark.
- Tools like SynthID (Google), Imatag, Truepic are pushing this for outputs.
- Combine with weight provenance: not just "is this AI?" but "from whose model? under what license?"

### Royalty distribution
- If a brand uses a licensed LoRA in a campaign, generation count → royalty to the LoRA owner.
- Today: nobody collects this. It's a leaky bucket.
- On-chain settlement (Imperium) makes this transparent and instant.

### Synthetic talent / persona licensing
- "Lil Miquela but with proper IP infrastructure."
- An artist licenses their persona LoRA to a brand for one campaign. Brand gets the LoRA gated behind your inference API. Royalty per generation. Watermarked output. Time-bounded.
- This is the **consented deepfake economy.** Smaller market today, very large in 5 years.

### Personal AI agents
- Your digital twin: voice + face + writing style + knowledge.
- Boveda is already shaped for this.
- Today: hobbyist. Tomorrow: every public figure has a licensed AI agent for fan engagement, brand deals, education content.

## 5. The ultimate business idea

**"Stripe for AI Licensing"** built on top of the post-vanity ecosystem the user is already building.

### Shape

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  LoRA Vault (your ecosystem extension)                     │
│                                                            │
│  - Boveda            = registry (already exists)           │
│  - StanVault         = identity / biometric verification   │
│  - Imperium          = royalty settlement on-chain         │
│  - Tizita            = visual taste curation               │
│  - Crucibla          = release readiness scoring           │
│                                                            │
│  + new layer: VAULT  = inference-as-a-service for weights  │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
                           ↓ API only, weights never leave
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Buyers                                                    │
│  - Brands, agencies, studios                               │
│  - Buy access tokens. Tokens carry license terms.          │
│  - Calls hit Vault inference API. Outputs are watermarked  │
│    with provenance + buyer + timestamp.                    │
│  - Royalty per generation flows on-chain to artist.        │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Why this wins

1. **Weights never leave the vault.** Solves the core leak problem. Buyers can't redistribute what they don't possess.
2. **Provenance is built in.** Each artifact carries a manifest signed at training time. Buyers can audit.
3. **Royalties auto-settle.** No manual invoicing, no chase. Imperium handles it.
4. **License is enforced in code, not just in contract.** Time-bounded tokens, geographic restrictions, generation-count caps.
5. **Identity is verified.** StanVault confirms the buyer is who they say (KYC for AI use) and the artist consented (right-of-publicity proof).
6. **Outputs are watermarked.** Misuse is detectable.

### Revenue model

- 5% take rate on every licensed generation (Stripe-like).
- Premium tier for enterprise: dedicated infra, SLA, audit logs, compliance reports.
- Annual subscription for "bring your own LoRA" creators (vault hosting + watermarking + royalty rails).
- Marketplace listing fee for buyers searching the registry.

### Unfair advantages the user already has

- **Built ecosystem, not greenfield.** Tizita, Boveda, Imperium are real. Most competitors are starting from zero.
- **Artist-first lens.** Coming from the artist side means the rights story is honest, not extractive. This is increasingly rare and increasingly defensible.
- **Cultural specificity.** African / diaspora-first positioning (Oryx, etc.) means a beachhead market that incumbents (Adobe, OpenAI) ignore.
- **On-chain settlement is already in your stack.** Imperium kills the "who owes whom" friction that breaks every traditional licensing scheme.

## 6. Go-to-market shape

Phased, smallest beachhead first:

### Phase 1 — single-artist proof (you, Ubani)
- Build the vault for your own LoRAs.
- One brand pilot: a label, a fashion brand, or an agency licenses Ubani for one campaign.
- Watermarked outputs. Royalty paid via Imperium. Document the case study.

### Phase 2 — invited artists
- Five to ten Afrobeats / amapiano artists you have direct relationships with.
- They onboard their LoRAs to your vault. Brand pilots with each. Network effects: brands value the catalog more than any single LoRA.

### Phase 3 — open registry, gated quality
- Open onboarding for any artist with verified rights. Curation gate to keep junk out.
- Marketplace surface for buyers. Search + audit + license.

### Phase 4 — enterprise tier
- Direct contracts with Fortune 500 brand-safe AI buyers.
- Dedicated infra, audit packs, compliance docs.
- This is where 80% of revenue ends up.

## 7. What to NOT build

Useful negative space:

- **A general-purpose LoRA marketplace.** Civitai exists. Race-to-the-bottom for hobbyists. Don't compete there.
- **A foundation model.** Capital intensive, OpenAI and Anthropic dominate. You ride on top of them.
- **A consumer-facing AI image generator.** Midjourney, Krea, etc. own this. You provide infrastructure to creators.
- **NFT marketplace for LoRAs.** Did not work in 2021-22, will not work now. Royalty rails matter; speculative collectibles do not.

---

## 8. The single most defensible moat

Not the LoRA. Not the model. Not even the inference API.

**The provenance ledger.** Once you own the registry of "who trained what, on what data, with what consent, under what license, generating which outputs, for which buyer, paid out when, watermarked how" — you become the trust layer for an entire industry. Switching costs become enormous. Network effects compound. Regulators eventually require something like this; whoever owns the de-facto standard wins.

Build the ledger first. Everything else is downstream of it.
